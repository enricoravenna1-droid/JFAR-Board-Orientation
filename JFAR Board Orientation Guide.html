Section 1 of 8 · about 4 minutes

1. General overview

The Jewish Federation of Arkansas exists to strengthen, support and ensure the future of the Jewish community in Arkansas, and to provide for the Jewish people in Israel and worldwide. That is the actual mission language written into our bylaws, and every decision this board makes should trace back to it.

JFAR serves an estimated 8,000 to 12,000 Jewish Arkansans statewide, with a deliberate push to grow our presence in Northwest Arkansas. Enrico Ravenna became Executive Director in August 2025, when the organization was largely dormant. Since then, the Executive Director's role has centered on connecting this community to the people, partners and opportunities that put Jewish Arkansas on the map, and building the relationships that make the next program, the next partnership and the next milestone possible. In one year, programming roughly tripled and the board grew to the size our bylaws allow. Neither of those things happened by accident, and neither will what comes next.

The Executive Director's role is to build the bridges: the relationships, the partnerships and the vision for what this community becomes next, plus the daily execution that turns it real. Erin Cohen runs community engagement and Northwest Arkansas outreach. Wendy Paquette runs the office. A fractional technology team and an outsourced accountant round out the operation. None of that replaces what directors bring. The board does not just approve what gets built here. Directors lead, and carry the power to create just as much as staff does. That partnership is the subject of section seven.

Why this guide exists: our bylaws assign a specific subcommittee, Board Development and Nominating, chaired by the Vice President, the job of improving this board through orientation, education, leadership development and assessment. This guide is that job, done once and done well, so you are not left relying on a hallway conversation to learn how this board actually works.

Key takeaways

  • Mission first. Every vote should trace back to it.
  • The board leads and creates. Staff builds bridges and executes daily. Both matter, neither replaces the other.
  • This guide fulfills a bylaws obligation. It is not a courtesy.
  • Your progress saves automatically. Work at your own pace.

Source: JFAR Bylaws, Article 1 (mission and purpose) and Article 4, Section 4.2 (Board Development and Nominating subcommittee).

2. Board member responsibilities and expectations

Every nonprofit director in the country answers to the same three legal duties. Learn them once and you will recognize them behind almost every rule in this guide.

Duty of care

Show up informed. Read what is sent before the meeting, not during it. Ask the question you actually have, not the safe one. The legal standard is the care an ordinarily sensible person would use running their own affairs, applied here to ours.

Duty of loyalty

Federation interests come before personal ones. If a vote touches you, your business or your family, say so out loud before the vote, not after. Our bylaws require a conflict of interest policy for exactly this reason, and prohibit any form of self dealing outright.

Duty of obedience

Follow the mission, the bylaws and the law, including the federal rules that come with 501(c)(3) status. That also means no discrimination on the basis of gender, sexual orientation, race or national origin, in this room or in how JFAR shows up anywhere else.

What that looks like at JFAR, in numbers

Our bylaws cap this board at 20 voting members, and we currently sit at that ceiling. Terms run two years, renewable for up to three terms or six consecutive years, after which a director sits out for two years before returning. We also seat non voting clergy members representing Jewish congregations across the state, a standing reminder that this board answers to the whole community, not just its own room.

Every director is expected to serve on at least one of our seven standing committees, covered in section five. Governance here is not a spectator activity.

Key takeaways

  • Care, loyalty, obedience: the three duties behind every rule in this guide.
  • Disclose conflicts before the vote, not after.
  • 20 seats is the ceiling. Two year terms. Six years maximum before a required break.
  • Committee service is expected, not optional.

Source: JFAR Bylaws, Article 1, Sections 1.5 and 1.9. Article 2, Sections 2.2 and 2.5. Article 6, Section 6.7. Duty framework per BoardSource and the National Council of Nonprofits (see Resources).

4. Meeting procedures and attendance

Our bylaws set a floor of quarterly board meetings. In practice, this board meets monthly, evenings, currently 7:00 to 8:30 PM Central over Google Meet. The President chairs. The Secretary calls the roll, keeps the minutes and distributes them within two weeks.

Quorum and voting

A quorum is a simple majority of voting directors, including at least two officers. Without it, no vote counts. Once a quorum is present, a majority of those in the room, in person or on the call, decides the matter. No director holds veto power, regardless of title or tenure.

You may vote by proxy, in writing or electronically, but a proxy expires after one month. The board can vote by email between meetings for most matters, but only after a genuine attempt to meet first, and never to change the bylaws, seat a new board member, or approve a large financial request above the bylaws threshold. Those three always require an actual meeting.

How agendas are built

JFAR labels every agenda item INFORM, DISCUSS or DECIDE, so you know before you walk in whether an item needs your vote or just your attention. Materials go out in advance. Read them before the meeting, not during it, per the duty of care in section two.

Attendance

Three consecutive absences from regular meetings can be treated as cause for removal, decided by a two thirds vote of directors present with due notice. If you need to resign, do it in writing. If you are missing meetings, call the Board President or the Executive Director before it becomes a pattern.

Executive session

Reserved for staff matters or a removal discussion. What is said in executive session stays there. The board may also vote anonymously by majority vote when the matter calls for it.

Key takeaways

  • Quarterly is the legal floor. Monthly is what we actually do.
  • Quorum needs a majority of directors and at least two officers.
  • INFORM, DISCUSS, DECIDE tells you what an item needs from you before the meeting starts.
  • Three consecutive absences can trigger removal. Speak up before it gets there.

Source: JFAR Bylaws, Article 2, Sections 2.3 through 2.11. Article 3, Section 3.6.

5. Bylaws

You do not need to memorize ten pages of legal language. You need to know what is in them and where to find the full document when a real question comes up. Here is the walk through, article by article. Click any one to expand it.

Article 1: Name, purpose and powers

Sets our mission, our 501(c)(3) status and the federal rules that come with it: no self dealing, no private benefit, no political campaigning.

Article 2: Board of Directors

Board size (13 to 20 members), meeting cadence, terms, quorum and voting. Covered in detail in sections two and four.

Article 3: Officers

President, Vice President, Secretary and Treasurer, each elected for two year terms, capped like directors at three terms. If the President cannot serve, the Vice President steps in, then the Treasurer, then the Secretary, in that order.

Article 4: Committees

Seven standing committees: Executive, Budget and Finance, Campaign, Social Services, Endowment and Investment Fund, Governance, and Programming. The Executive Committee is the officers plus the outgoing President, and its Board Development and Nominating subcommittee is who built this guide.

Article 5: Contracts, loans, checks and deposits

Any loan or contract above a set threshold needs a board resolution first. That rule exists so no single signature can put the Federation at risk.

Article 6: General provisions

Covers our fiscal year (July 1 through June 30), the annual accountant review, the conflict of interest mandate, and the director and officer insurance covered in section three.

Article 7: Amendment

A proposed change has to be raised at one meeting and sit until the next one before the board can vote on it. Nothing changes on the spot. Our bylaws have been amended eight times since 2013, most recently in March 2025. That is what a living governance document looks like.

Governance terms worth knowing

Quorum

The minimum number of voting directors who must be present for the board to legally act. At JFAR, a majority of directors including at least two officers.

Proxy

Written or electronic authorization letting someone else cast your vote when you cannot attend. Expires after one month at JFAR.

Ex officio

A seat held by virtue of another role, rather than by election. JFAR's clergy seats are ex officio and do not vote.

Fiduciary duty

The legal obligation to act in the organization's best interest. Covered in full in section two: care, loyalty and obedience.

Executive session

A closed portion of a meeting, used for staff matters or a removal discussion, where discussion stays with the board.

The Executive Director or the Office Manager can send you the complete, current bylaws (last amended March 3, 2025) any time you want the exact legal language behind any of this.

Key takeaways

  • Seven articles cover everything from mission to amendment procedure.
  • Amendments require two meetings, never a same day vote.
  • The full current document is one email away.

Source: JFAR Bylaws, Articles 1 through 7 in full, as amended March 3, 2025.

6. Community engagement

Programming at JFAR roughly tripled this past year, from close to nothing to about 18 programs and more than 700 community engagements. Numbers like that do not come from the office alone. They come from a board that shows up.

Some of what that looked like this year: A Night to Honor Israel, an educational Shabbat and Mimouna celebration with Temple Shalom, a partnership with Crystal Bridges, and a Community Shabbat Dinner with University of Arkansas Hillel. None of it happened because one person planned it alone. It happened because board members, staff and community partners, including congregations statewide, interfaith partners like Agape Church and Family Church Bryant, and national partners, showed up together.

Security is part of this too, and it is one of our flagship strengths. JFAR is a flagship partner of the Secure Community Network, and we have completed security assessments for synagogues statewide. Community engagement without a security posture behind it is not engagement. It is exposure.

Your role

You are not a messenger. You are a builder. Every board seat carries real power: to open a door, help plan an event, and create a piece of what this community becomes next. That means showing up to events, introducing the Federation to people who should know it exists, taking on programs as real deliverables you own rather than tasks you simply attend, and saying yes when there is a door only you can open. This board gets stronger every time a member's relationships and ideas become part of what this community can do. Yours does the same.

Key takeaways

  • Programming tripled because the board engaged, not just the office.
  • Security and community engagement are the same job, not two separate ones.
  • You are a builder here, not a messenger. Own a deliverable, not just an invitation.

Source: JFAR FY 25/26 Progress Report (internal document).

Section 7 of 8 · about 4 minutes

7. Taking initiative and working with the Executive Director

Board leadership and staff leadership are not a hierarchy. They are two engines pointed at the same mission. The Executive Director's role is to build the bridges: the relationships, the partnerships and the vision for what this community could become next, plus the daily execution that makes it real. The board's role is not to watch that happen from the sidelines. It is to lead alongside staff: plan events, open doors only directors can open, and grow what this community reaches. Directors carry the power to create here, the same as staff does. Our bylaws describe the structure behind this directly: the board and officers retain professionals, an Executive Director among them, to carry the mission forward. That is not a courtesy. It is the actual structure.

Twenty board members giving twenty individual instructions to a four person staff is not leadership. It is chaos with a letterhead. So here is the actual path for a good idea: bring it to the relevant committee first, since committees are where board level work happens between full meetings, or bring it straight to the Executive Director if it is time sensitive. Either way, it goes through one channel, not five, so your idea gets real backing instead of getting lost.

This protects the staff who make the work happen, and it protects your idea. A good idea that skips the process becomes a directive nobody voted on. A good idea that goes through committee becomes something the whole board owns, and something you get to see through as a deliverable, not just a suggestion.

When something needs to stay quiet

Staff performance, personnel matters and anything sensitive belong in executive session, covered in section four. What is said there stays there. That is not secrecy. It is how you protect people while still doing the job.

Key takeaways

  • Board leadership and staff leadership are two engines, not a hierarchy.
  • You lead, plan and create here. This is not a spectator seat.
  • New ideas go through committee or straight to the Executive Director, never straight to staff.
  • One channel protects staff and turns your idea into a deliverable the whole board owns.

Source: JFAR Bylaws, Article 3, Section 3.9. Article 4, Section 4.2.

8. Fundraising responsibilities and expectations

You did not join a book club. You joined the board of a fundraising organization, and fundraising is a board job, not just a staff job. Full board giving participation is the standard we hold ourselves to, every year, no exceptions.

Structurally, this runs through the Campaign Committee. Per our bylaws, the Board President appoints a Campaign Chair every year, at least one board member serves on that committee, and the money it raises gets used exactly as the Budget and Finance Committee recommends and the full board approves. No one, staff or board, spends a campaign dollar on their own authority.

What that asks of you personally: make a meaningful gift yourself, every year, before you ask anyone else to. Then use what you actually have, which is relationships, not a script. Introduce a peer who should know us. Host a handful of people in your living room. Share what JFAR is doing on your own channels. Say yes when the Executive Director or the Campaign Chair asks you to make one specific ask to one specific person.

Funders notice board giving before they notice anything else we say about ourselves. A board that gives is a board that believes what it is asking others to believe. That is the whole case.

Key takeaways

  • Fundraising is board work, not spectator work.
  • Give first, then ask. That order matters.
  • Your relationships are the ask. Use your own network before you use a script.

Source: JFAR Bylaws, Article 4, Section 4.4. Ambassador framing per BoardSource and the Association of Fundraising Professionals (see Resources).
